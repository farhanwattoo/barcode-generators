Over 10 billion barcode scans happen every day around the world. Barcodes are the invisible infrastructure behind modern commerce, healthcare, and logistics — yet most people only notice them at the supermarket checkout. This guide covers every major industry that uses barcodes, which barcode types they rely on, and why barcodes are essential to each sector.

Retail and point of sale

Retail is where barcodes were born. The first commercial barcode scan took place at a Marsh supermarket in Ohio in 1974, and today virtually every consumer product sold in a physical store carries a barcode. The retail use case is the most familiar: a cashier or self-checkout scanner reads the barcode on a product, and the POS system instantly retrieves the price, product name, and updates the inventory count.

🛒

UPC-A and EAN-13 — the universal retail barcodes

UPC-A (12 digits) is the standard in the United States and Canada. EAN-13 (13 digits) is used everywhere else — Europe, Asia, Australia, Latin America. Every product sold in a mass-market retail environment needs one of these barcodes to be stocked and sold through standard POS systems. Both are issued under the GS1 GTIN standard. GS1 Sunrise 2027 is enabling retailers to also accept 2D barcodes at checkout by 2027.

E-commerce and fulfilment

E-commerce operations rely on barcodes at every stage of the order lifecycle — from receiving inventory into a warehouse, to picking and packing individual orders, to generating shipping labels.

  • Receiving: Workers scan EAN/UPC barcodes or SSCC barcodes on pallets to update inventory counts in the warehouse management system.
  • Storage: Warehouse shelf locations are labelled with Code 128 or QR barcodes. When stock is put away, workers scan both the product barcode and the shelf location barcode.
  • Picking: Pickers scan barcodes to confirm they have taken the correct item from the correct location, preventing errors.
  • Packing and shipping: The completed order triggers a shipping label with a carrier barcode (Code 128) that tracks the parcel from despatch to delivery.

Warehousing and logistics

The global logistics industry processes billions of parcels, pallets, and containers every year — and barcodes are the primary technology that makes tracking possible at scale.

📦

Code 128 and GS1-128 on shipping labels

Every shipping label from FedEx, UPS, DHL, and national postal services carries a Code 128 or GS1-128 barcode encoding the tracking number. Scanners at depots and sorting facilities read these barcodes as parcels pass on conveyors — updating the tracking record in real time. Outer shipping cartons use ITF-14 barcodes that print reliably on rough corrugated cardboard. Pallets use SSCC (Serial Shipping Container Code) barcodes — 18-digit GS1-128 codes giving every pallet a globally unique identifier.

Healthcare and pharmaceuticals

Healthcare is one of the most regulated barcode environments in the world because barcode errors here can directly harm patients. Barcodes are used on patient wristbands, medication packaging, blood bags, surgical instruments, and laboratory specimens.

Patient safety and medication administration

Scanning a patient's wristband barcode before administering medication is a standard clinical workflow known as Bedside Medication Verification (BMV). The nurse scans the patient's wristband, then scans the medication barcode. The system verifies it is the right drug, right dose, right route, and right time for that specific patient. Studies have demonstrated that barcode-assisted medication administration significantly reduces wrong-patient and wrong-medication errors.

💊

Pharmaceutical serialisation

Under the US Drug Supply Chain Security Act (DSCSA) and the EU Falsified Medicines Directive (FMD), prescription drug packaging must carry a 2D Data Matrix barcode encoding the product's GTIN, serial number, batch number, and expiry date. This enables verification that the medicine is genuine and has not been tampered with at any point in the supply chain.

Manufacturing and quality control

Manufacturing uses barcodes to track components, work-in-progress, and finished goods through every stage of the production line. Every scan creates a data record that supports quality control, traceability, and compliance.

Direct part marking (DPM)

In aerospace, automotive, and electronics manufacturing, barcodes must survive the full lifetime of the product. Instead of labels, Data Matrix barcodes are permanently marked directly onto metal and plastic parts using laser engraving, dot peening, or electrochemical etching. These Direct Part Marks are scannable for the life of the component and cannot be lost or removed. Manufacturing execution systems (MES) use barcode scans at each workstation to record which operations have been performed on each unit — essential for automotive recalls, aerospace audits, and ISO quality certifications.

Libraries

Libraries were among the earliest adopters of barcodes, implementing them in the 1980s to automate book loan records. Today, virtually every library worldwide uses barcodes on their books and on borrower cards. Books carry two barcodes: the publisher's ISBN barcode (an EAN-13 on the back cover) and the library's own internal Code 39 or Code 128 barcode that links to the library's catalogue record. When a borrower checks out a book, the librarian scans the borrower card barcode and then the book barcode — a two-scan process that records the loan in under a second.

Events and ticketing

Barcodes on tickets serve two purposes: verifying authenticity and preventing duplicate use. Entry gate scanners read the barcode and instantly check it against a central database of valid, unused ticket codes.

🎟️

QR codes and PDF417 on modern tickets

Printed tickets and mobile tickets stored in Apple Wallet or Google Wallet use either QR codes or PDF417 barcodes. QR codes are increasingly preferred for mobile tickets because they scan reliably from phone screens. PDF417 remains standard for airline boarding passes per the IATA BCBP specification. European transit systems including Amtrak, Deutsche Bahn, and Eurostar use Aztec codes for train and bus tickets.

Food and agriculture

Barcodes in the food industry serve two critical functions: retail sale identification (the standard EAN/UPC barcode on every food product) and supply chain traceability (tracking food from farm to table for safety and recall purposes). Fresh produce, meat, fish, and bakery products use GS1-128 barcodes that encode the GTIN alongside Application Identifiers for batch/lot number, pack date, best-before date, and net weight. This structured data enables supermarkets to perform targeted product recalls — removing only the specific batch from a specific farm on a specific date, rather than pulling all stock of a product.

Asset tracking

Any organisation that owns significant numbers of physical assets — computers, tools, medical equipment, vehicles, office furniture — can use barcodes to manage them. Each asset gets a barcode label, and an asset register links each barcode to the asset's description, purchase date, value, location, and service history. Asset tracking barcodes are typically Code 128 or QR codes printed on durable polyester or metallic labels that resist scratching, chemicals, and UV exposure.

Marketing and advertising

QR codes have become a mainstream marketing tool following the explosion in smartphone QR scanning from 2020 onwards. Brands place QR codes on product packaging, print advertisements, posters, business cards, restaurant menus, and billboards to drive consumers to digital destinations. Common uses include links to product pages, promotional offers, digital menus, contact cards (vCards), loyalty programme accounts, and customer reviews. Dynamic QR codes — where the destination URL can be changed after printing — enable marketers to track scan volumes and update campaigns after materials are already in the market.
